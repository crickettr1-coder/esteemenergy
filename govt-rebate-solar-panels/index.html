What Is the Govt Rebate for Solar Panels?

The term govt rebate for solar panels can be a little misleading. It doesn’t refer to a cash-back scheme or a cheque from the government. Instead, it's a market-driven discount that comes from the Federal Government’s Small-scale Renewable Energy Scheme (SRES).

When you install a solar power system, it generates Small-scale Technology Certificates (STCs). Each certificate represents a portion of renewable energy that your system will generate over its lifetime. These certificates have a market value, and the amount generated depends on your system size, location (solar zone), and the year of installation.

Your solar provider usually handles the process and subtracts the value of your STCs from your final invoice—making it appear as a rebate or discount. The result is the same: you pay significantly less upfront.

So, while it's not a direct government payment, the govt rebate solar panels program effectively reduces the installation cost by thousands for eligible Australian households.

How the Federal STC Program Works

The STC program is at the heart of the govt rebate solar panels initiative. Here's how it works in detail:

  • When you install a solar power system under 100kW, it qualifies for STCs under the Federal Government’s Small-scale Renewable Energy Scheme.

  • The number of STCs your system creates is based on:

    • The size (kW rating) of your system

    • Your geographic location (solar zone 1 to 4)

    • The expected output over the remaining years of the scheme (ends in 2030)

  • Each STC has a fluctuating market price, typically between $30 and $40.

  • Installers typically claim the certificates on your behalf and apply them as an upfront discount at the point of sale.

This process makes the system more affordable immediately, which is why the govt rebate solar panels scheme has helped over 3 million Australian homes go solar.

Keep in mind:

  • The number of STCs created reduces by one-fifteenth each year, meaning the rebate value decreases annually.

  • The market price of STCs also fluctuates, affecting how much you'll save.

These STCs are essentially solar subsidies, encouraging early adoption and rewarding homeowners who switch to renewable energy sooner rather than later.

How Much Can You Save with Solar Rebates in 2025?

In 2025, the average Australian household can still access substantial savings through the govt rebate solar panels program. The exact amount depends on your system size and location, but the savings can easily reach into the thousands.

Example:

  • A standard 6.6 kW system might cost around $8,500 before rebates.

  • Based on STC values and solar zone, you could receive a rebate of approximately $2,000 or more.

  • That brings the net cost down to around $6,500—a significant reduction.

Factors affecting your rebate value:

  • Location: Northern, sunnier regions (like Darwin or Townsville) receive more STCs.

  • System Size: Larger systems generate more renewable energy and thus more certificates.

  • STC Price: The market value of certificates can vary, so timing can affect savings.

This rebate structure means the earlier you install solar, the more you stand to gain. And the long-term benefit? Massive solar savings on energy bills, often recouping your investment within 3 to 5 years.

Are There Other Government Incentives or Solar Panel Grants?

Yes, in addition to the federal govt rebate for solar panels, many Australian states and territories offer their own solar panel grants and government incentives to make solar more accessible:

  • Low-Income Household Rebate Swap: Eligible customers can swap their energy rebate for a fully installed 3 kW system.

  • Energy Savings Scheme (ESS): Provides support for upgrading to energy-efficient appliances and systems.

These programs work in addition to the govt rebate solar panels, helping reduce upfront costs even further.

Important Considerations Before Claiming Rebates

While the govt rebate solar panels scheme makes solar more affordable, there are important details to be aware of before committing:

  • Accredited Installers Only: You must use a Clean Energy Council (CEC)-accredited installer to be eligible for STCs.

  • Product Standards: Your panels and inverters must be approved and meet Australian quality and safety standards.

  • Timing Matters: Each year the number of STCs you can claim decreases, reducing your potential rebate. Installing now locks in higher savings.

  • Market Volatility: STC prices are not fixed. Delaying your installation could mean receiving fewer dollars back, depending on certificate value.

  • New Regulations: With the rise of two-way electricity pricing and export charges, it’s essential to consider how these may impact your long-term solar ROI.

Planning ahead ensures you get the most from your system—and your rebate.

To get the most value from the govt rebate for solar panels, follow these smart steps:

Frequently Asked Questions​

What government rebates are available for solar panels in Australia?

 The primary rebate is through the federal STC program, offering upfront discounts for eligible solar installations. State-based programs also offer additional incentives like interest-free loans, battery rebates, and solar grants.

How much can I save with the govt rebate for solar panels?

In 2025, a typical 6.6 kW system can attract a rebate of around $2,000, depending on your location and current STC prices.

How do I apply for the solar rebate?

 You don’t need to apply. Your installer handles the STC paperwork and subtracts the rebate from your installation cost automatically.

Can renters access solar rebates or incentives?

 Renters can’t claim STCs directly, but some states offer trials and landlord incentive schemes that enable shared benefits between tenants and property owners.

What happens if the STC price drops before I install?

 A lower STC price reduces your upfront rebate. That’s why installing sooner—when STC prices are higher—is better financially.

Is there an income limit to qualify for the solar rebate?

 The federal rebate (STC program) has no income test. However, state-based programs like NSW’s Low-Income Household offer are income-based.

Do solar batteries qualify for government rebates?

 Not under the federal STC program, but several states—such as SA, VIC, and NSW—offer specific rebates or loans for battery storage.

What is the difference between the solar rebate and a feed-in tariff?

 The solar rebate reduces your system’s upfront cost. The feed-in tariff (FiT) pays you for excess electricity exported to the grid after installation.
