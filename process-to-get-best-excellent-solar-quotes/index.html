History of Solar Power Technology

Thomas Edison and Nikola Tesla had differing views on the best approach for electrical power distribution. More information in context to this:"

Direct Current (DC):

Thomas Edison was a proponent of direct current (DC) electricity. In a DC circuit, electrons flow in one direction, from the positive terminal to the negative terminal of a power source. Edison's early work with electricity and his development of the incandescent light bulb were closely tied to DC systems. While DC systems were suitable for short distances, they faced challenges when transmitting electricity over longer distances due to significant energy losses.

Alternating Current (AC):

Nikola Tesla, on the other hand, championed alternating current (AC) electricity. AC involves the flow of electrons that periodically change direction, rapidly oscillating between positive and negative cycles. One of the key advantages of AC systems is that transformers can be used to change the voltage of the current. This capability made it possible to step up the voltage for efficient long-distance transmission and step it down again for safer use in homes and businesses. AC's ability to easily transform voltage made it more suitable for transmitting electricity over greater distances, reducing energy losses.

In the late 19th century, Edison and Tesla had a notable rivalry, often called the "War of Currents." Edison was deeply invested in promoting DC electricity, while Tesla and his financial backer, George Westinghouse, were proponents of AC. The rivalry extended beyond the technical merits of each system and involved business and marketing tactics.

In the end, AC electricity proved more practical for long-distance power transmission, which became crucial as electrical grids expanded and power generation centralised. The development of efficient transformers, which allowed for voltage conversion and, thus, long-distance transmission, played a significant role in the success of AC systems. As a result, AC eventually became the dominant choice for power distribution.
