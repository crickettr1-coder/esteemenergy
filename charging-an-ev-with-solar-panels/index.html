Process of EV charging using solar

Solar power is the process of collecting solar energy through a solar panel and then changing that light into electricity with the use of the photovoltaic effect. These panels are ordinarily mounted on roofs or other special structures of solar farms, delivering electricity in the form of direct current (DC). In order to fit this solar energy into electric vehicle chargers, there is a final process known as inverter conversion. The inverter comes into the electricity direct current (DC) AC, which is common with most AC chargers for electric vehicles. Since the AC electricity has been generated from the inverter, the next step is to connect it to the EV charger. These chargers come in different levels: There is level 1, which can use any regular 15- or 20-amp 120-volt outlet; level 2, which requires its own circuit; and level 3, which is a direct-current charger. The EV charger controls the amount of current supplied to the EV battery to prevent any danger or inconvenience that may be caused by excessive current. To charge the EV, one has to connect the charging port of the EV to the charger connector; the electricity generated by the solar power system charges the battery of the EV as it flows through the charger. To manage this, manufacturers have provided systems and software that can check the amount of energy produced by solar power, energy consumed, and the charge of batteries. This optimization helps charge a battery during the day when there is plenty of sun and not rely on the grid at night or even when there is an outage. Notably, the charging duration may still differ because of variations in the capacity of the electric vehicle’s battery, the charger’s rate, and the amount of solar power that can be tapped into. If the amount of collected solar energy is insufficient, the charging system can obtain electricity from the network if the car is still charged.
