8. What Is a Purchase Power agreement?

A Purchase Power Agreement(PPA) is an agreement between a customer and a third party where they both agree to install and operate a solar system on the owner’s property. The customer agrees to purchase electricity the system generates at a fixed rate but at different periods.

9. What are STCs?

Scale Technology Certificate (STC) is a renewable energy certificate (REC) used to offset the cost of initializing solar systems.

10. What is a feed-in Tariff (FIT)? 

FFeed Tariff (FIT) is a govt mandate price utilized to pay for electricity generated by REC. It is a rate that the electricity retailer pays for any surplus electricity that the solar system produces. In Australia, the government has set a minimum mandatory price of $0.10/kWh.

12. Are 25-Year Performance Warranties For Solar Panels Normal? 

Yes, 25-year performance warranties for solar panels are common and have become an industry standard. These warranties typically guarantee that the solar panels will maintain a certain level of performance over 25 years. Performance warranties usually specify that the panels will still produce a minimum percentage of their rated power output after a certain number of years. Remember that the performance of solar panels can be affected by various factors, including weather conditions, shading, and maintenance. Regular maintenance and proper installation by qualified professionals can contribute to your solar panel system's optimal performance and longevity.

19. How long will it take to charge my EV at home?

The time it takes to charge an electric vehicle (EV) at home depends on several factors, including the charging power of your home charging station, the capacity of your EV's battery, and the state of charge when you begin charging. Here are the key factors to consider:

Charging Power (kW): 

The charging power of your home charging station, typically measured in kilowatts (kW), is a critical factor. Common home charging stations for EVs have power ratings ranging from 3.6 kW to 22 kW or more. The higher the power rating, the faster the charging.

Battery Capacity (kWh): 

The capacity of your EV's battery, measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh), determines how much energy it can store. EVs have different battery capacities, and larger batteries take longer to charge.

State of Charge (SOC): 

If you're starting to charge your EV from a low state of charge (e.g., 20%), it will take longer to charge compared to when starting from a higher state of charge (e.g., 80%).

25. Does the solar system come with insurance?

Solar panels and solar power systems typically come with warranties to protect against manufacturing defects and ensure the performance of the equipment. However, warranties are not the same as insurance. Let's break down the distinctions:

Warranties: 

  • Manufacturer's Warranty: Solar panels typically come with a manufacturer's warranty covering defects in materials and workmanship. This warranty usually lasts 10 to 25 years, with most reputable manufacturers providing a linear performance guarantee over the warranty period.
  • Inverter Warranty: Inverters, a crucial component of a solar power system, also have warranties. These warranties typically range from 5 to 25 years, depending on the type and brand of the inverter.
  • Workmanship Warranty: Solar installers often provide a separate workmanship warranty for the installation itself. This warranty covers issues related to the installation process and is typically valid for 1 to 10 years.

Insurance:

  • While warranties cover equipment-related issues, they do not cover accidents, theft, vandalism, or other unforeseen events that may damage or destroy the solar system. For these types of risks, homeowners may want to consider solar insurance.
  • Solar Insurance: Some insurance companies offer specialised solar insurance policies. These policies can cover the cost of repairing or replacing solar panels and related equipment in the event of covered perils. It's important to carefully review the terms and coverage of any insurance policy to understand what is included and excluded.
  • Homeowners Insurance: In some cases, homeowners may be able to add coverage for their solar panels to their existing homeowners insurance policy.

30. How to clean solar panels?

Cleaning solar panels is important to ensure optimal performance and maximise energy production.  Here are steps you can take to clean solar panels effectively:

Check Manufacturer's Guidelines: 

Before cleaning, check the manufacturer's guidelines and warranty information for specific recommendations on cleaning and maintenance. Some manufacturers may provide specific instructions or precautions.

Safety First:

Ensure your safety by following proper safety procedures. If the solar panels are on the roof, use appropriate fall protection measures and avoid working on wet panels to prevent slipping.

Time Your Cleaning: 

Plan to clean the solar panels early in the morning or late in the afternoon to avoid cleaning them in direct sunlight when they are hot. Cleaning hot panels may lead to streaking due to rapid drying.

Use a Soft Brush or Sponge: 

For ground-mounted systems, use a soft brush or sponge with a long handle to reach the panels. If the panels are on the roof, you may use a soft brush attached to a long pole.

DIY Cleaning Solution: 

Mix a mild soap or dishwashing detergent with water in a bucket. Use a solution of one part soap to ten parts water. Avoid using abrasive or harsh chemicals, as they may damage the panels.

Apply Soapy Water: 

Dip the soft brush or sponge into the soapy water solution and gently scrub the surface of the solar panels. Focus on areas with visible dirt, bird droppings, or other contaminants.

Rinse with Clean Water: 

Rinse the solar panels thoroughly with clean water after scrubbing. A hose with a gentle spray nozzle can be used for this purpose. Make sure all soap residue is washed away.

Squeegee or Soft Cloth: 

Use a squeegee or a soft, non-abrasive cloth to remove excess water and ensure a streak-free finish. Make sure the cloth or squeegee is clean to avoid introducing scratches.

Repeat as Needed: 

Depending on your location and environmental conditions, you may need to clean your solar panels regularly. In areas with minimal dust and pollution, cleaning a few times a year may be sufficient. In more dusty or polluted environments, more frequent cleaning may be necessary.

Monitor Performance: 

After cleaning, monitor your solar system's performance to ensure that energy production returns to expected levels. If you notice a significant drop in performance, it may indicate other issues that require attention.
