All About Hybrid Solar Systems

2023-10-26 09:09:28•By EsteemEnergy_AU
In this article, we will explore the topic of hybrid solar systems. The hybrid solar system is a type of solar power system that is made of the combination of solar panels with other features (which usually are to improve the efficiency and reliability of the system) to make the power system highly effective. Unlike the PV systems of the grids or off-grids, a hybrid system combines energy storage options using batteries as the mainstays for availing the excess energy at a later time. The integration of energy storage facilities results in the system's operation in grid mode and off-grid modes; as a result, the system could take advantage of all the benefits that both grid-tied and off-grid systems could provide.

Key components of a hybrid solar system include:

 1. Solar Panels: Photovoltaic (PV):

The bare ledge is packed with solar cells for collecting sunlight or turning it into electricity.

 2. Inverter:

An inverter plays a crucial role in the process of DC(Direct Current) electricity produced by solar cells, into AC(Alternating Current), which is necessary for commercial and residential purposes.

3. Battery Storage:

When used as a renewable energy reserve, energy storage is not only a key element of hybrid systems. Batteries use that time to charge when the sun is out, and energy can be freed during periods of low solar input, night-time hours or when the load is heavy.

4. Charge Controller:

It controls the circulation and charging of electricity from solar panels to batteries to avoid the batteries or achieving a full charge.

5. Grid Connection:

It is provisioned with the electrical grid; it is able to pull electricity from the grid when solar energy is low, and it pushes extra solar energy back to the grid when production exceeds demand.

6. Backup Generator:

Hybrid systems may embrace a backup generator that uses diesel or propane, a conventional fuel type that will give additional energy during likely cases like low sunlight or high power demand.

 Pros and cons of a hybrid solar system

Pros of a Hybrid Solar System:

1.  Grid Independence:

With hybrid systems, we can gain virtually uninterrupted power, so the user can create, store, and use electric energy.

2.  Energy Storage:

The integration of the battery makes possible energy storage, supporting users in using the additional production during hours of no solar radiation.

3.  Backup Power:

A combined system may give some backup power during grid failure or when the system experiences peak demand, which, in turn, may be enhanced by the installation of a generator.

4.  Increased Self-Consumption:

The users, hence, can raise their power generation by conserving the excess energy and reducing their grid power usage during peak times.

5.  Grid Export and Credits:

Extra power not being used can be sold back to the grid, and consumers might get paid for the electricity they provide during peak hours.

6.  Environmental Impact:

Decreased dependence on the grid alongside managing stored power supply helps build a more environment-friendly system, which in turn supports renewables.

 Cons of Hybrid Solar System:

1. Complexity and Cost:

Installed hybrid systems will require more equipment and have become more complex and expensive than a simple solar generator with only the battery due to adding other components.

2. Maintenance:

Laptops, computers, and similar devices are not immune to various issues, which may require regular maintenance and add up to a higher overall price.

3. Limited Battery Lifespan:

The batteries of hybrid systems are designed to last around 5 to 15 years, which imitates a considerable cost and reduces reliability.

4. Space Requirements:

The installation of storage batteries requires additional space because the reserve system needs to be installed and space needs to be designed severely.

5. Efficiency Losses:

While transforming from DC to AC and charging and discharging batteries leads to some energy losses, efficient energy management is still necessary to enhance the usefulness of renewable energy resources.

 6. Dependency on Sunlight:

All solar systems including hybrid systems, depend on the sun as an energy source. The result of ephemeral light will also impact system performance.

7. Technology Advancements:

Advancements in the field of solar power and energy storage might result in constantly evolving technologies with consecutive upgrades just after combining both technologies.

How Does a Hybrid Solar System Work?

During the daytime, a hybrid solar system operates by harnessing sunlight to generate electricity through solar panels. Here's a step-by-step explanation of how a hybrid solar system works during daylight hours:

1. Solar Panel Power Generation:

The solar or photovoltaic (PV) panels are to be installed either on the roof or in an open area, depending on the season.  By doing so, the sunlight is collected. The DC generated by the sun rays, when falling on the solar cells in the panel, is stored as DC electricity.

2. Inverter Conversion:

The DC generated is then connected to an inverter where the AC is produced. Most home appliances are produced with AC power, with only the electrical grid running on DC power.

3. Powering the Load:

The electrical energy through AC converters light up the household and facility devices and electrical appliances. The solar panels, having harvested the sunlight during the daytime, supply electricity in a way that would efficiently represent the electricity demand of the premises at that time of the day.

4. Battery Charging:

In a hybrid power system with battery storage, while an excess of electricity generated by the solar panels is directed into the batteries and their charge, the excess generated by the solar panels is directed into the batteries. Such a situation is an outcome when solar power generation is higher than the capacity of the building to take the excess power at that particular moment in time.

5. Grid Interaction:

The central function of a hybrid node-grid system is feeding back the excess energy that is not used immediately or kept in batteries to the grid. In this case, a bidirectional meter becomes the main instrument used to perform the process; the mode of the flow of electricity is measured for both directions.

 6. Net Metering:

Certain regions have net metering programs that recognize and reward the energy users for the excess electricity they contribute to the grid by giving them credit for it. If solar generation is less than the credit, the same credit is used to balance the electricity consumption from the grid into the reverse or at night.

 7. Continuous Monitoring and Optimization:

The hybrid solar system that comprises the photovoltaic panels is monitored and optimized by an energy management system (EMS) and oscillates between the solar panel and batteries throughout the day. The EMS regularly performs energy management tasks in such a method as to make electricity generated by solar panels, most effectively, stored or sent back to the grid in dependence on demand and prevailing solar weather conditions.

 8. Load Powering and Battery Discharge in the Evening:

Together with the sun going behind the horizon and solar panels producing less energy, the hybrid system may take power from the batteries to keep the power supply satisfactory once the batteries are discharged, the system will instantiate permission to get power from the grid.
